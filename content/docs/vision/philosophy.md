# Philosophy

The entries below are the axiomatic values W3DS commits to. An entry can be challenged only by arguing the value is wrong, not by citing a mechanism it would forbid. [Principles](../principles) name the architectural commitments that honor these values.

---

## 1. Sovereignty over personal data requires both ownership and legibility

Personal data originates with a person and belongs to that person; platforms are visitors, never possessors. Any authority another actor holds over that data is legitimate only insofar as it is visible to the subject. Ownership without legibility is a nominal claim; legibility without ownership is audit on someone else's property.

## 2. Identity is continuity across services

Who a person is should outlast the services that happen to know them. An identifier that a platform can revoke, rename, or lose is not an identity; it is an account. The word "identity" names a continuity the subject carries, not a record a service keeps.

## 3. Plurality of implementation is a public good

Many independent implementations collaborating is preferable to one canonical system everyone must adopt. Diversity of implementation is treated as a public good to protect, not a coordination tax to minimize.

## 4. Legitimate delegation is bounded and revocable

Control that the subject cannot recall, or that they never agreed to in this form, is not control. Irrevocable delegation is a category error; so is treating a past yes as present license beyond the shape of that yes.

## 5. Meaning must outlast any particular actor

A user's records must remain legible and authoritative after the platforms, vendors, and standards that produced them are gone. Meaning is carried in the record itself, not borrowed from parties who might withdraw.

## 6. The past is part of the record

Every action on a user's data leaves a trace; the trace is part of the data, not a side note that an operator may keep or discard. What happened is the primary record; the current state is a projection of it. There are no silent edits, no backfilled history, no accounts of past action that rest on trust rather than evidence.

## 7. The open web is a commons

Commons are sustained by contribution, not enclosure. W3DS builds on what exists: shared vocabularies, interoperable formats, linked-data semantics. New standards are minted only where existing ones leave a genuine gap. A parallel stack is not extension — it is fragmentation.
